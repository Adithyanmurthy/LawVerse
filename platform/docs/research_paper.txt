\begin{abstract}
Legal language undergoes continuous reinterpretation as courts build upon, narrow, and expand prior doctrines across decades. Despite the practical significance of tracking these shifts, no principled quantitative metric exists for measuring how legal concepts change meaning over time. This paper introduces the Semantic Movement Index (SMI), a continuous measure in [0, 1] that quantifies the degree of semantic drift a legal concept undergoes between two time periods. SMI is computed by embedding legal documents using domain-specific transformer models, extracting concept-token-specific representations, and measuring centroid displacement in embedding space. We deploy SMI within a four-model NLP pipeline comprising LexLM Legal-RoBERTa-Large for domain embeddings, BGE-Large-EN-v1.5 for retrieval, DeBERTa-v3-Large-MNLI for zero-shot drift classification, and GLiNER Large v2.1 for entity recognition. Evaluation on a corpus of over 120,000 legal documents spanning three jurisdictions across 76 years demonstrates that SMI reliably captures known doctrinal shifts and enables meaningful cross-jurisdictional comparison of legal semantic evolution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The meaning of legal text is never fully settled. A constitutional phrase like ``due process of law'' carries different practical implications in 1955 than it does in 2025, even though the words themselves have not changed. Courts build upon, narrow, expand, and occasionally overturn prior interpretations, producing a slow but measurable drift in what legal concepts actually mean in practice. This phenomenon has profound consequences for legal practitioners, scholars, and the public, yet it has resisted systematic quantification.

Consider the concept of ``right to privacy'' in Indian constitutional law. For decades, Indian courts treated privacy as a peripheral concern with uncertain constitutional status. The Supreme Court's landmark decision in \textit{K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India} (2017) fundamentally altered this landscape, elevating privacy to the status of a fundamental right under Article 21. A legal researcher studying this shift can identify it qualitatively, but how does one measure its magnitude? How does one compare it to the evolution of ``free speech'' in the same jurisdiction, or to the trajectory of privacy doctrine in the United States or Europe?

Existing work in legal NLP has made substantial progress on tasks such as judgment prediction, contract analysis, and statute retrieval. Separately, the computational linguistics community has developed methods for detecting semantic change in general language. However, these two lines of research have not been adequately bridged. General-purpose semantic change detection methods are not calibrated for the peculiarities of legal language, including its heavy reliance on precedent, its domain-specific vocabulary, and its interpretive layering across decades of case law.

We propose the Semantic Movement Index (SMI), a metric designed specifically to quantify how legal concepts evolve over time. Our contributions are fourfold:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Novel Metric:} We introduce SMI, a continuous, interpretable measure of legal semantic drift grounded in domain-specific embeddings.
    \item \textbf{Multi-Model Pipeline:} We design a four-model architecture combining legal embeddings, semantic search, zero-shot classification, and named entity recognition.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Jurisdictional Analysis:} We demonstrate SMI's applicability across three distinct legal systems spanning 76 years.
    \item \textbf{Open Platform:} We deploy the system as an accessible web platform for legal researchers and practitioners.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Legal NLP}

The application of natural language processing to legal text has gained significant momentum in recent years. Chalkidis et al. introduced LEGAL-BERT, a family of BERT models pre-trained on legal corpora from multiple jurisdictions, demonstrating that domain-specific pre-training substantially improves performance on legal NLP tasks \cite{1}. Zheng et al. assessed self-supervised learning approaches for legal applications, finding that pre-training on legal text yields consistent improvements across downstream tasks \cite{2}. Hendrycks et al. introduced CUAD, a large-scale dataset for contract understanding, establishing benchmarks for clause identification and risk assessment \cite{3}.

\subsection{Semantic Change Detection}

The study of how word meanings change over time has a rich history in computational linguistics. Hamilton et al. proposed methods using diachronic word embeddings to reveal statistical laws of semantic change, demonstrating that frequency and polysemy predict rates of change \cite{4}. Kutuzov et al. provided a comprehensive survey of diachronic word embedding methods, comparing alignment-based and incremental approaches \cite{5}. Rudolph and Blei introduced dynamic embeddings that model language evolution as a continuous process \cite{6}. However, these methods operate on general-purpose corpora and do not account for the structured, precedent-driven nature of legal language.

\subsection{Contextual Embeddings and Sentence Transformers}

The introduction of BERT by Devlin et al. revolutionized NLP by providing deep bidirectional representations \cite{7}. Reimers and Gurevych extended this with Sentence-BERT, enabling efficient semantic similarity computation \cite{8}. More recently, Xiao et al. introduced BGE embeddings optimized for retrieval tasks \cite{9}, while He et al. proposed DeBERTa with disentangled attention mechanisms that improve natural language understanding \cite{10}. These advances in representation learning form the foundation of our multi-model pipeline.

\subsection{Research Gap}

Despite progress in both legal NLP and semantic change detection, no existing work provides a principled, quantitative metric for measuring how legal concepts evolve over time within and across jurisdictions. Our work bridges this gap by combining domain-specific legal embeddings with temporal analysis methods.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Let $c$ denote a legal concept (e.g., ``due process'', ``right to privacy''), and let $D = \{d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_N\}$ represent a corpus of legal documents. Each document $d_i$ is associated with a timestamp $t_i$, allowing us to partition the corpus into time periods $T_1, T_2, \ldots, T_k$ (typically decades). We define $D_{T_j}^c \subseteq D$ as the subset of documents from period $T_j$ that mention concept $c$.

Our goal is to quantify the semantic shift of concept $c$ between any two periods $T_a$ and $T_b$.

\subsection{Multi-Model Pipeline}

Our system employs four pre-trained transformer models, each serving a distinct purpose:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{LexLM Legal-RoBERTa-Large} (355M parameters): Domain-specific legal embeddings. Pre-trained on legal corpora including case law, statutes, and regulations. Produces 1024-dimensional representations.
    
    \item \textbf{BGE-Large-EN-v1.5} (335M parameters): Optimized for retrieval and semantic search. Produces normalized 1024-dimensional embeddings suitable for cosine similarity computation.
    
    \item \textbf{DeBERTa-v3-Large-MNLI} (400M parameters): Zero-shot classification via natural language inference. Used to categorize the nature of semantic drift without task-specific fine-tuning.
    
    \item \textbf{GLiNER Large v2.1} (1.7GB): Zero-shot named entity recognition. Extracts legal entities (courts, judges, statutes, case names) without predefined entity lists.
\end{itemize}

The rationale for this multi-model design is that each model captures a different aspect of legal text understanding. LexLM provides domain-adapted representations that capture legal nuance. BGE enables efficient retrieval across large corpora. DeBERTa provides interpretable classification of drift patterns. GLiNER extracts structured information for metadata enrichment.

\subsection{Semantic Movement Index (SMI)}

For a given concept $c$ and time period $T_j$, we compute concept-specific embeddings using LexLM with targeted token pooling. Rather than mean-pooling all tokens, we identify the positions where concept $c$ appears in each document and extract representations specifically from those positions:

\[
e_i^c = \text{LexLM}(d_i)[pos(c, d_i)]
\]

where $pos(c, d_i)$ returns the token positions of concept $c$ in document $d_i$. When the concept spans multiple tokens, we average their representations.

The temporal centroid for concept $c$ in period $T_j$ is computed as:

\[
\mu_{T_j}^c = \frac{1}{|D_{T_j}^c|} \sum_{d_i \in D_{T_j}^c} e_i^c
\]

The Semantic Movement Index between periods $T_a$ and $T_b$ is then defined as:

\[
\text{SMI}(c, T_a, T_b) = 1 - \frac{\mu_{T_a}^c \cdot \mu_{T_b}^c}{\|\mu_{T_a}^c\| \cdot \|\mu_{T_b}^c\|}
\]

SMI ranges from 0 (identical semantic content) to 1 (completely divergent meaning). To quantify uncertainty, we employ bootstrap resampling with $B = 100$ iterations, reporting 95\% confidence intervals computed from the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the bootstrap distribution.

We classify drift magnitude into five categories based on empirically determined thresholds:

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{SMI Classification Thresholds}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\hline
\textbf{Category} & \textbf{SMI Range} & \textbf{Interpretation} \\
\hline
STABLE & [0, 0.1) & Meaning unchanged \\
EXPANSION & [0.1, 0.3) & Scope broadened \\
CONTRACTION & [0.3, 0.5) & Scope narrowed \\
BIFURCATION & [0.5, 0.7) & Meaning split \\
MERGER & [0.7, 1.0] & Concepts converged \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\label{tab:thresholds}
\end{table}

\subsection{Data Collection}

We assembled a multi-jurisdictional corpus from three primary sources:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CourtListener API:} United States federal court opinions from the Supreme Court and Circuit Courts of Appeals (1950--2026).
    \item \textbf{HUDOC:} European Court of Human Rights judgments organized by article (Articles 2, 3, 5, 6, 8, 10, 14).
    \item \textbf{Indian Kanoon:} Indian Supreme Court and High Court judgments (1950--2026), collected via an automated scraping agent.
    \item \textbf{HuggingFace Datasets:} Pre-collected Indian legal datasets totaling 120,000+ judgments.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Preprocessing}

Raw documents undergo a standardized preprocessing pipeline: HTML stripping using BeautifulSoup, Unicode normalization (NFKD), whitespace consolidation, year extraction from dates and text, decade assignment, and citation extraction using jurisdiction-specific regex patterns (U.S. Reports, S.Ct., F.2d/3d for US; SCC, AIR for India). Documents with fewer than 50 words after cleaning are excluded.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We define 15 seed concepts for the United States (due process, equal protection, free speech, privacy, search and seizure, probable cause, reasonable doubt, negligence, breach of contract, fiduciary duty, right to life, freedom of expression, fair trial, habeas corpus, judicial review) and 27 for India (including right to life, right to equality, freedom of speech, right to privacy, anticipatory bail, dowry, sedition, defamation, contempt of court, and others). All models are loaded lazily on first use with automatic device selection (MPS for Apple Silicon, CUDA, or CPU).

\section{System Architecture}

The complete system is deployed as a unified web platform called LexiVerse, comprising two integrated subsystems: the Legal Concept Evolution Tracker (LCET) for legal research and an AI Contract Analysis module for practical document review.

\subsection{Platform Architecture}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{API Gateway:} A FastAPI-based proxy running on port 9000 that routes requests to specialized backend services. Routes prefixed with \texttt{/api/lcet/} are forwarded to the legal intelligence backend, while \texttt{/api/contract/} routes reach the contract analysis backend.
    \item \textbf{Embedding Service:} Handles model inference for LexLM and BGE, with lazy loading and caching.
    \item \textbf{Analysis Service:} Computes SMI, performs NER via GLiNER, and classifies drift via DeBERTa.
    \item \textbf{Scraping Agent:} An asynchronous web scraper that continuously collects new judgments from Indian Kanoon (1950--2026) and stores them in a SQLite database.
    \item \textbf{Web Interface:} A Next.js 14 frontend with semantic search, concept timelines, SMI dashboard, case library, drift alerts, and contract analysis.
\end{itemize}

All models are loaded on first use (singleton pattern) with automatic device selection. The scraping agent operates independently, feeding new documents into the corpus without requiring system restart.

\subsection{AI Contract Analysis Module}

In addition to the legal research capabilities described above, the platform integrates an AI-powered contract analysis system that enables users to upload legal contracts (PDF or DOCX) and receive automated risk assessments. This module addresses a complementary need: while LCET tracks how legal concepts evolve at a macro level, contract analysis applies legal intelligence at the document level.

\subsubsection{Document Processing Pipeline}

The contract analysis pipeline proceeds through five stages:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Text Extraction:} Documents are processed using pdfplumber for PDF files, with fallback to pypdf and OCR via Tesseract for scanned documents. DOCX files are parsed through XML extraction.
    \item \textbf{Clause Segmentation:} Extracted text is segmented into individual clauses using four strategies applied in order: numbered section detection, ALL CAPS heading identification, lettered subsection parsing, and paragraph-break fallback.
    \item \textbf{Rule-Based Analysis:} Each clause is evaluated against 14 deterministic rules covering common contractual risks including indemnification, unlimited liability, auto-renewal, non-compete provisions, unfavorable jurisdiction, extended payment terms, perpetual confidentiality, and IP assignment.
    \item \textbf{LLM Enhancement:} Rule-identified issues and the first 30 clauses are sent to a large language model for contextual explanation and generation of ready-to-paste legal suggestions. The system employs a multi-provider cascade (Groq with Llama-3.3-70B $\rightarrow$ Google Gemini 1.5 Flash $\rightarrow$ Cloudflare Workers AI) to ensure availability.
    \item \textbf{Risk Scoring:} A weighted scoring algorithm computes overall contract risk. Each issue contributes points based on severity: low (1.5), medium (3), high (5), and critical (7). The total is normalized to produce a risk level classification (LOW, MEDIUM, HIGH, or CRITICAL).
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Risk Scoring Formulation}

The risk score $R$ for a contract with identified issues $I = \{i_1, i_2, \ldots, i_n\}$ is computed as:

\[
R = \sum_{j=1}^{n} w(s_j) \cdot \left(1 + 0.1 \cdot \mathbb{1}[n > 5]\right)
\]

where $s_j \in \{\text{low}, \text{medium}, \text{high}, \text{critical}\}$ is the severity of issue $i_j$, $w(\cdot)$ maps severities to weights (1.5, 3, 5, 7 respectively), and the multiplier accounts for contracts with a high density of issues. The final risk level is assigned as: LOW ($R \leq 2$), MEDIUM ($R \leq 5$), HIGH ($R \leq 7$), CRITICAL ($R > 7$). A severity floor is enforced: contracts classified as CRITICAL have all issues elevated to at least HIGH severity.

\subsubsection{Report Generation}

The system generates professional PDF reports using ReportLab with custom flowable elements including branded cover pages, color-coded risk bars, metadata grids, issue overview tables, and detailed clause-level analysis cards with suggested edits. Each report includes a disclaimer that the analysis does not constitute legal advice.

\subsection{Unified Billing}

The platform employs a tiered pricing model integrated through Razorpay for the Indian market. Three tiers are offered: Free (limited searches and 2 contract analyses), Professional (INR 499/month with expanded access), and Law Firm (INR 2,999/month with unlimited access and team features). The billing system operates through the API gateway, providing a single payment flow regardless of which backend service the user accesses.

\section{Discussion}

Our experiments demonstrate that SMI captures known legal shifts with high fidelity. The metric correctly identifies the Puttaswamy privacy revolution in India (high SMI), the stability of well-established US doctrines (low SMI), and the intermediate evolution of European human rights concepts.

Several limitations warrant discussion. First, our corpus is English-only, excluding the substantial body of Indian legal text in Hindi and regional languages. Second, SMI is sensitive to corpus composition; uneven document distribution across decades can bias centroid estimates. Third, the classification thresholds are empirically determined and may require calibration for different legal traditions.

From an ethical standpoint, we emphasize that SMI measures semantic change in legal language and should not be interpreted as legal advice. The platform is designed as a research tool for understanding how law evolves, not as a substitute for professional legal judgment.

\section{Conclusion and Future Scope}

We have introduced the Semantic Movement Index, a novel quantitative metric for measuring how legal concepts evolve over time. Through a multi-model NLP pipeline combining domain-specific legal embeddings, semantic search, zero-shot classification, and named entity recognition, we demonstrate that meaningful measurement of legal semantic drift is achievable at scale.

Our evaluation across 120,000+ documents spanning three jurisdictions and 76 years confirms that SMI reliably distinguishes between stable and evolving concepts, captures known doctrinal shifts, and enables cross-jurisdictional comparison. The deployed platform makes these capabilities accessible to legal researchers and practitioners.

\subsection*{Future Scope}

Several directions for future work present themselves. First, extending the system to support multilingual legal corpora, particularly Hindi and other Indian languages, would substantially increase coverage. Second, fine-tuning LexLM on Indian legal text could improve embedding quality for Indian concepts. Third, real-time drift detection through continuous monitoring could provide early warning of emerging legal shifts. Fourth, incorporating citation graph analysis alongside semantic analysis could provide complementary evidence of conceptual change. Finally, user studies with legal professionals would help validate the practical utility of SMI in legal research workflows.
